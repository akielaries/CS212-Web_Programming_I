Fahrenheit 451 Analysis

Author's Background

Ray Bradbury, born on August 7, 1920 and died on June 6, 2012, was a science fiction author who wrote novels that shaped the 20th and 21st century for their evoke of themes of censorship, threats of technology, racism, etc. Bradbury integrated his hobby for reading pulp magazines with his own speculations about the future in a style of poetic metaphors. He was able to be a fluid and successful writer without higher education; instead he read as many works as he could to exercise his imagination. These included works from Edgar Allan Poe, Ernest Hemmingway, and Thomas Wolfe. He expressed his appreciation for literature in his book Fahrenheit 451 in 1953. Fahrenheit 451 dealt with a world without literature, where every person thought alike and were prohibited to have outstandingly different views on matters of life. He was heavily influenced to write this book with inspiration from his childhood’s events; from living during World War Two to his relationship with his grandfather. Bradbury also wrote short stories that were included in “The Martian Chronicles” and “The Illustrated Man” where he touched on themes of the interaction of society with technology and psychology of people. The Martian Chronicles set creatures from Mars on Earth who try to find a way to live through conflicts among people from Earth. After Fahrenheit 451, Bradbury’s reputation allowed him for opportunities for new outlets for his imagination. He has been honored by his “contributions to American literature” (Jonas).

Social Context

Bradbury created the title of the book from the temperature at which paper burns : 451℉. The dystopian novel sets a time where people are prohibited to own or read books. “Firefighters” burn any book that they find or that people hide away. This is done to stop the spread of new ideas to keep people ignorant and controlled. Inspired by the censorship by the Nazi in WW2, Bradbury used this event in history to produce a story to expand on the rise of dictators who want to take complete control of their country and people’s mind. The rise of technological advances in the 20th century plays a major theme in Fahrenheit 451 to develop the possible threats that these advancements can force society to hide away from literature. The book hints that technology could be a distraction controlled by the government to keep the people ignorant and away from the “danger” of books. His work also warned citizens to fear the spread of communism in the U.S. by infiltrated communists in society who encouraged censorship. The novel accurately describes technologies half a century prior to our time now that TV and phone screens exist.

Major Themes

Censorship:

Literature is heavily censored and all books are banned and burned, the crux of Fahrenheit 451’s overarching story; how a fireman’s job has flipped from saving the world from fires into ridding the world of books and literature by setting them on fire. Indeed, Ray Bradbury feared such a notion of censorship was already well under way within the real world, as he displays through Montag's initial disposition of how he has never questioned the status quo (Bahrani). Yet, as the story goes on and Montag begins to transform, through such transformation, Bradbury asserts the glimmer of hope that's still present in the face of extreme censorship.

Knowledge vs. Ignorance:

The entirety of the story's plot revolves around the constant struggle between knowledge and ignorance. How it is the firemen's job to destroy books, promoting ignorance; how there is a secret network of people all over the country who remember pieces of literature, retaining books' value as repositories of knowledge; how Montag's inner conflict between ignorance and knowledge is resolved by those promoting knowledge, as they ignite a spark of doubt in Montag, resulting in knowledge being the victor via Montag's newfound pursuit of knowledge.

Role of Technology:

Bradbury paints the innovations in technology to be the catalyst for society’s issues, as such innovations have fostered a mass addiction to entertainment. How Montag accredits his wife’s emotional disconnection to technology, as she remains plugged into some form of entertainment at all times. Furthermore, nuclear technology becomes the be-all and end-all, as an atomic bomb destroys everything Montag once knew; indeed, Bradury asserts the notion of technology’s egregious, inherent destructive aspect, as it destroys entire societies along with the spread of knowledge.

Dissatisfaction:

Another factor that may be accredited to the social decline of reading is dissatisfaction. Of course, such dissatisfaction ties back in with the role of technology and censorship: how censorship has promoted the dissatisfaction with books being overwhelming and too offensive; and how technology has fostered a constant need for more, better, and innovative ways of consuming entertainment.

Analysis of Major Characters

Guy Montag:

Mostly known by Montag, Guy Montag is the main character of Fahrenheit 451. Montag is an ordinary civilian in the alternate American Society that Ray Bradbury built for this novel; he burns books for no other reason than: “Books are bad!” However, when Montag witnesses an innocent lady burned with her books, Montag begins to fall away from society.

Captain Beatty:

The antagonist, the anti-Montag — along with everyone else in society — and the embodiment of censorship in Fahrenheit 451, Captain Beatty, thought to be a trustworthy acquaintance and boss, Montag finds that Beatty is his greatest enemy. From one perspective, Beatty’s character is a metonymy for government; for, the government is never actually seen or referred to, Beatty plays into the government’s efforts to keep people away from text that will inflict emotion or thinking or promote superiority in society by trying to keep Montag form reading and dispelling the insides to the rest of the society. From another perspective, Beatty acts as a child playing with a couple of bugs, or their rodent pet, constricting Montag’s range of wander. Everytime Montag begins to consider the idea of books and the effects of reading, Beatty slaps his hand down, closing off Montag’s pathway, forcing Montag to return to his place and find a different path.

Clarisse McClellan:

Clarisse, she is only around for a short time, but she leaves a lasting impression. Clarisse is the first character we meet. Considering the world around her, she is crazy, abnormal, non-conformant to the expectations of society, and it is the first time that Montag meets Clarisse that his perception begins to change; however, with Clarisse’s death, solidifies Montag’s perception of something being wrong with the world, and, more importantly, the burning of books.

Professor Faber:

Introduced half way through, Professor Faber is the essential piece to make Montag realize that there is something very wrong with burning books; consequently, Faber is the aid that brings Montag to expose this problem in society in a negative fashion: Having firemen, just doing their jobs, lose their houses and wives, and whatever else, because they do not understand; contrary to the philosophies of Granger.

Mildred Montag:

Known as Millie to Guy Montag, Mildred is Montag’s wife; however, as the book progresses, Mildred becomes more of a husk than the woman she once was to Montag. An empty body who has been consumed by the fast pace of the society, and unwilling to consider the possibility of stepping away to think and process. Considering this, Mildred functions as support for Bradbury’s assertion on the role of technology: Giving into the control that technology has gained on citizens.

Granger:

Granger, introduced at the end, proposes to Montag that his reading and his escape were not in vain, for humanity will end itself and will have to restart, and those who survived the end will be there to pass down the knowledge with held in books to the generations to come.

Analysis of Major Literary Devices

“It was a pleasure to burn. It was a special pleasure to see things eaten, to see things blackened and changed.” (Bradbury 3).

Immediately, Bradbury presents the reader with the main point of contention within the dystopian world he has created: how the society of such a world perceives book burning, the spread of ignorance, as a pleasure. Such an immediate introduction to this dystopian premise, then, establishes the story’s overarching gloomy, hopeless tone, as Bradbury asserts his fears of the real-world society being gripped by the promotion of ignorance (Bahrani). Furthermore, Bradbury uses the futuristic premise to his advantage, as the ever lingering threat of another nuclear war, due to the innovative advances in nuclear technology, fosters the apocalyptic atmosphere, furthering the intensity of the tone. And as the story’s tone intensifies, so do its prevalent how: how the symbolisms of fire take hold of the story’s constant conflict between ignorance and knowledge.

Fire presents itself countless times, with differing undertones. The beginning of the book, Montag loves the qualities of fire. Mid way through, he experiences the destructive properties; sorrow would best characterize this undertone. Near the end, when Montag runs away, he destroys his own home and kills his team of firemen. The most notable quality of these events is fire’s destruction, and the use of fire by man; Bradbury plays off this image to foreshadow Granger’s comment at the end. Humanity is shown burning its pyre, and the surviving groups are a metaphor for the Phoenix, rising from the ashes, reconstructing his pyre for the next few hundred years.
